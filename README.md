## 🎯 Project Overview

This project transforms a raw hospital patient dataset into a **single-page interactive Excel dashboard**, built to give hospital administrators a fast, visual read on patient demographics, admissions, treatment outcomes, and revenue — without needing to open a single row of raw data.

The workbook follows a clean **three-layer structure**:

1. **QUESTIONS** — the 10 core business questions the analysis was designed to answer
2. **SOURCE DATA** — the cleaned, patient-level dataset (15 records, 16 fields)
3. **PIVOT TABLE** — 10 pivot tables, one per business question, feeding the dashboard visuals
4. **DASHBOARD** — the final interactive, slicer-driven visual layer

This structure means the workbook is not just a dashboard — it's a fully auditable analytical trail from raw record to final KPI.

## 🗂️ Dataset Overview

- **Records:** 15 patients (Patient IDs P0001–P0015)
- **Fields:** 16 columns spanning demographics, clinical, financial, and outcome data
- **Grain:** One row per patient encounter
- **Time span:** Admission dates ranging across multiple discharge cycles, with Length of Stay calculated per encounter

This is a **compact, illustrative dataset** — small enough to validate every dashboard number by hand against the raw records (which was done for this report; see [Detailed Findings](#-detailed-findings)), while still being large enough to demonstrate a complete BI workflow from raw data to insight.

## 🧹 Methodology

1. **Data Cleaning (Power Query)** — the raw patient records were loaded into Power Query, where data types were standardized (dates, numbers, text), column names normalized, and the dataset checked for blanks, duplicates, and inconsistent category labels (e.g., ensuring "Insurance" was spelled identically across all rows).
2. **Pivot Table Construction** — 10 separate pivot tables were built directly off the cleaned `SOURCE DATA` sheet, each isolating the metric needed to answer one of the 10 business questions.
3. **Dashboard Assembly** — pivot tables and pivot charts were arranged into a single dashboard canvas, with KPI cards built from key pivot outputs and two global slicer panels (left and right) added for interactivity.
4. **Validation** — every headline KPI on the dashboard (Total Revenue, Average Length of Stay, Mortality Rate, Average Satisfaction Score) was independently recalculated from the 15-row source data for this report and confirmed to reconcile exactly with the dashboard's displayed values.

## 🔎 Detailed Findings

All figures below were recalculated directly from the 15-row source dataset to verify the dashboard's outputs.

### Demographics
- **Gender split:** 8 Male (53%) / 7 Female (47%) — a near-even distribution
- **Age distribution:** 28–37 is the largest single bracket (4 patients), followed by 48–57 (3 patients); the 68–77 and 78–87 brackets each have just 1 patient — confirming the hospital's patient base skews toward the working-age population

### Departments
- **Patient volume:** Cardiology and Neurology are tied for the highest volume (4 patients each), followed by Orthopedics (3), with Geriatrics, Gynecology, Oncology, and Pediatrics at 1 patient each
- **Satisfaction by department:** Gynecology and Pediatrics recorded the highest average satisfaction (10.0 each), followed by Neurology (8.75) and Orthopedics (8.33); Geriatrics recorded the lowest average satisfaction (5.0)

### Financials
- **Total revenue:** $76,850 across 15 encounters — an average bill of **$5,123** per patient
- **Insurance vs. non-insurance billing:** Insurance-billed encounters carry a dramatically higher average bill (**~$9,017**) than cash (**$500**) or credit card (**$900**) encounters — consistent with insurance covering the hospital's highest-acuity cases (surgery, chemotherapy, stroke, cancer)
- **Payment method mix:** Insurance is the dominant payment method (8 of 15 patients, 53%), followed by Cash (4, 27%) and Credit Card (3, 20%)
- **Insurance provider mix:** Aetna, Blue Cross, and Cigna each cover a portion of the insured patients, with Aetna and Blue Cross tied as the most-used providers (3 patients each)

### Clinical Outcomes
- **Length of stay:** Average of 5.6 days, ranging from 2 days (minor fractures, early pregnancy checks) to 15 days (oncology chemotherapy) — confirming that the average masks a long tail of resource-intensive cases
- **Mortality:** 2 of 15 patients (13.3%) died — both were **emergency admissions with extended stays** (a stroke patient with a 5-day stay, and a pneumonia patient with a 7-day stay) — reinforcing the link between emergency presentation, longer stays, and mortality risk
- **Readmission:** 4 of 15 patients (26.7%) were readmitted — notably, **all 4 readmitted patients were also Insurance-paying patients with above-average bill amounts**, suggesting readmissions cluster among more complex, higher-cost cases
- **Emergency admissions:** 4 of 15 patients (26.7%) were admitted as emergencies; 2 of these 4 emergency patients were also among the hospital's 2 mortality cases — a 50% mortality rate within the emergency subgroup versus 0% among non-emergency patients in this dataset

## 💡 Key Insights & Narrative

**A balanced but working-age-skewed patient base.** With a near-even gender split (53% male / 47% female) and the largest age cohorts falling between 28 and 57, this hospital's patient base is dominated by the economically active population rather than pediatric or elderly care — even though both segments (Pediatrics, Geriatrics) are represented.

**Insurance is subsidizing the hospital's most complex cases.** The average insurance-billed encounter ($9,017) is more than **10x** the average cash encounter ($500), and every readmitted patient in this dataset was insurance-covered. This isn't a coincidence — insurance is the payment method attached to the hospital's highest-severity, highest-cost treatments (surgery, chemotherapy, stroke, cancer, and pneumonia care).

**Emergency admissions carry disproportionate clinical risk.** Emergency patients make up just 27% of admissions but accounted for **50% of all mortality cases** relative to their subgroup — despite mortality within the full dataset sitting at a modest 13.3% overall. This is the clearest early-warning signal in the dataset: emergency status, not volume, is where the hospital's clinical risk concentrates.

**Readmissions cluster among the same patients driving up cost.** All four readmitted patients were also high-bill, insurance-covered cases — pointing toward a testable hypothesis that discharge planning and follow-up care for complex, high-cost cases may need reinforcement.

**Length of stay hides a resource-concentration problem.** The 5.6-day average blends short 2-day stays (minor orthopedic and gynecological cases) with a 15-day chemotherapy stay — meaning a small number of patients are likely consuming a disproportionate share of bed-days and clinical resources.

## ⚠️ Operational Pressure Points

| Pressure Point | Metric | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Emergency admissions** | 26.7% of patients | Requires consistently ready triage and rapid-response capacity, and — in this dataset — carries a materially higher mortality risk than planned admissions |
| **Readmissions** | 26.7% of patients | Concentrated among high-cost, insured patients — signals a possible gap in discharge planning or post-treatment follow-up for complex cases |
| **Length of stay concentration** | 5.6-day average, up to 15 days | A small number of high-acuity cases (oncology, stroke, complex geriatrics) likely consume a disproportionate share of hospital resources |
| **Department satisfaction gap** | Geriatrics 5.0 vs. Gynecology/Pediatrics 10.0 | A 5-point satisfaction gap on a 10-point scale between the lowest- and highest-rated departments warrants a focused service-quality review in Geriatrics |

## ⚠️ Limitations & Caveats

- **Small sample size (n = 15):** This dataset is illustrative rather than statistically representative. Percentages (e.g., mortality rate, readmission rate) are highly sensitive to individual records — a single additional case can shift a rate by 6–7 percentage points. All findings should be read as **directional and demonstrative of methodology**, not as clinically generalizable conclusions.
- **Average Satisfactory Score KPI:** The dashboard KPI card displays a rounded value of **8**; the precise pivot-table average is **7.87 / 10**.
- **Department chart in the dashboard screenshot** appears to display only 4 of the 7 departments in the underlying data (Cardiology, Gynecology, Oncology, Pediatrics) rather than all 7 — this may be a rendering/cropping artifact of the exported view rather than the live filtered dashboard; the full department breakdown from the pivot table is provided in [Detailed Findings](#-detailed-findings) for completeness.
- **Causal language:** Observations such as the link between emergency admission and mortality, or between insurance billing and readmission, are **correlational observations within a 15-record dataset**, not statistically validated causal findings.

---

## 🧭 Recommendations

1. **Investigate the emergency-mortality link further** with a larger dataset to confirm whether emergency admissions carry a genuinely elevated mortality risk at this hospital, or whether this is an artifact of the small sample.
2. **Audit discharge and follow-up protocols** for high-cost, insurance-billed cases, given that all readmissions in this dataset fell into that segment.
3. **Run a service-quality review in Geriatrics**, where satisfaction (5.0/10) trails every other department by a wide margin.
4. **Expand the dataset** beyond 15 records to strengthen the statistical reliability of every rate-based KPI on the dashboard.
5. **Add a resource-utilization view** (e.g., bed-days by department) to make the "small number of patients consuming disproportionate resources" finding directly visible on the dashboard rather than inferred from length-of-stay averages.
